Language is a mental model
## **The impact of language on behavior**
The residents who live in the Guugu Yimithirr tribe of Australia do not have the concept of "front, back, left, and right" in their language. They only use absolute directions such as southeast, northwest, and northwest. Their judgment of the north-south direction is higher than that of other people, because daily communication in life needs to be based on the southeast and northwest.
> When we learn a language, in addition to increasing the vocabulary of the language, we also learn about the culture and way of thinking of the language, consciously or unconsciously.
For example, the Chinese sentence ""I eat"" can mean:
1. "I have eaten"
2. "I am eating"
3. "I'm about to eat" etc.
The word "eat" in English is "eat", which has 16 tenses (different tenses represent different time states)
For example:
1. Past tense (things done in the past): ate (I ate yesterday), past perfect tense: eaten (I ate)
2. Present continuous tense: eating (I am eating)
3. Future tense: will eat (I am going to eat), present simple: eats (I usually eat at this time)
When speaking English, you will consciously or unconsciously connect your movements with time. **It is easier to think about your own habits of this movement. In other words, it is easier to understand yourself**
In another example, an accident occurred in a museum and a man accidentally broke a vase.
English: He broke the vase (he broke the vase.)
Spanish would say: the vase broke, the vase broke itself (the vase broke.)
English speakers will remember who broke it, but Spanish speakers will not.
If it comes to assigning blame for the accident, English speakers will have a clearer idea of who to blame.
