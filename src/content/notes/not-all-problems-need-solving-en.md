In 1970, Zambian nun Mary Yokenda wrote a letter to Dr. Erst Sturinger of NASA.
In the letter, Sister Yokenda asked: How can he be willing to spend billions of dollars on a project as far away as Mars when there are so many children on earth who do not have enough to eat?
Scientist Ernst Sturinger wrote back to her, describing the role of space exploration in solving the problem of famine.
Citing the example of the development of microscopes, a count used the money used to relieve the people to fund the weirdo who was studying microscopes. Later, microscopes must have made more progress in medicine than using money to simply relieve the people.
To solve the problem of famine, we first need to do two things, one is food production and the other is food distribution.
The most ideal improvement in food production is actually artificial earth satellite. Data on rainfall, drought, etc. are communicated to the ground. Space exploration technology has also made a great contribution to the improvement of food, medical treatment, including industrial materials.
Space exploration has higher requirements for cooperation among countries, which can also improve the international relations of various countries.
> Many problems are solved by solving bigger problems. And bigger problems can bring more people to cooperate.
## **What are the important issues? **
### **Story 1**
Suppose you are the owner of an office building and your tenants complain that "the elevator is too slow and they have to wait for a long time every time, which is a waste of time. If they don't solve it, they will consider quitting the lease!" What is your solution?
If there are no restrictions, then the most direct solution is: **install a faster elevator**
But in fact, if you upgrade the elevator in your office building, other tenants will be affected during the maintenance period, and there will also be a lot of expenses.
Finally, the solution to the elevator problem is to install mirrors or mirrored elevators in the elevators, add TV ads in the elevator waiting area, and play pleasant music.
### **Story 2**
Here is another story about a baby incubator.
Many premature babies in the world must be placed in an incubator to survive because their bodies are not fully developed.
In the late 1870s, an obstetrician and gynecologist named Stephen Taney collaborated with zoo keeper O'Neill Martin and used the principles of a chicken incubator to create the first baby incubator in human history.
Although the incubator is a great invention, it has its drawbacks.
1. Very expensive. One unit costs US$40,000. Many poor countries cannot afford it.
2. The maintenance is complicated and requires professional technicians to repair it.
3. It consumes a lot of power and requires stable power supply.
Preistillo, a professor at MIT, discovered this problem. The local power supply was unstable, the humidity was high, and if it broke, it would not be repaired. Later, Preistillo decided to develop a new baby incubator for developing countries. He hoped that this incubator would be reliable, low-cost, and easy to maintain. The solution is to modify it with car parts. The main components are all from cars, and it can also be powered by motorcycle batteries. Best of all, your local auto mechanic can fix it in no time.

The story of the incubator is not over yet. Four graduate students at Stanford University have a graduate course called "Extremely Affordable Design."
The course task is to redesign the baby incubator.
When they arrived in Nepal, they found that Nepal could not afford an incubator. It was not because they had no money to buy one. Instead, most people live in rural areas with long distances to travel, and power outages are common in Nepal.
In the end, these four graduate students created a product that "requires no electricity" and is similar to a sleeping bag. It can be charged by just putting it in hot water. It can be used for 4 hours on a single charge and sells for only $25.
