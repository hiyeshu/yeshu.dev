## Destructive Communication Tools
Language is our most commonly used communication tool, but this tool itself has many flaws and is very inaccurate.
It brings us more opportunities for self-expression and communication, but it is also one of the causes of misunderstandings and conflicts between most people.
We have been trying to communicate with the outside world since we started babbling, but we rarely observe the characteristics of this tool, and we think less about how to use it.
And it is easy for us to classify "being good at using language" as having high emotional intelligence or being good at troubles. If we are not good at it, we can label it. Then use this as an excuse to cause misunderstandings and conflicts to continue.
In fact, many conflicts originate from harsh criticism of oneself, and this harsh criticism, due to inappropriate communication methods, will trigger a series of negative cycles, which will then intensify the harsh criticism of oneself.
We can try to compare "language" to a hammer. Using "language" is equivalent to using a "hammer".
### How to avoid violent use of tools?
> On the morning of last weekend, I didn’t do something very important. I was asked: Did you forget to do something important?
This was a very ordinary conversation, and it seems to be harmless now, but first I felt a sense of self-blame because I had forgotten these things, and then the self-blame began to turn to external complaints - do you know that I spend a lot of energy on other things? I was just busy and forgot about it, and it’s not like I really didn’t want to do it.
To carefully analyze your own mental journey is to regard the reminder as a kind of evaluation, and then blame yourself for forgetting important things.
When we judge ourselves, we distance ourselves from our own needs and are unable to take action to meet our needs. As a result, we feel depressed and lose touch with ourselves.
What I really want in my heart is a sentence of relief, but I feel too ashamed to express it, so it turns into another **very aggressive** communication method.
### Three solution steps
1. **Observe what happens. **Avoid analyzing and judging other people’s words and deeds. Analysis and comments can easily make you emotional.
2. **Express how you feel at the moment, such as sadness, fear, etc. **It is most likely that what we express here is evaluation rather than feeling. For example, in the above example, I was evaluating myself for not doing these things well, but my feeling was that I was distracted by other things, and I hoped to be forgiven. The more specific the feeling, the more understandable it is to oneself or others.
3. **Express how the emergence of feelings relates to yourself. **Due to culture and habits, we resist or resist telling the true reasons, and instead think that it is other people's reasons, because we are good at analyzing other people's reasons, but clearly expressing feelings has very little to do with ourselves. "I feel guilty, but because I only have one brain, I can't handle so many things." Such vague expressions can only make the other person feel that they are "unreasonable."
### **Learn to accept your imperfect self**
It’s easy to be hard on ourselves because we rarely get the chance to learn how to love ourselves.
The "love" in our education and family of origin is not given selflessly, for example, parents who "don't like eating meat."
Through "self-sacrifice", they do many things that they actually don't want to do to express "being good to you" and "loving you".
We don’t have a good way to learn how to love ourselves. After all, our parents don’t love us either.
Even only after learning "self-sacrifice" can I express "love you" and "be good to you", and then I will be harsh and ashamed of myself, which will turn into a kind of self-hatred.
In order to "self-sacrifice" and satisfy those who "self-sacrifice" for us, we will develop a habit of subconsciously judging ourselves and being very harsh on ourselves.
Therefore, we will think that we are not worthy of being loved and belong, and we will strongly deny ourselves, which will turn into "low self-esteem". We retreat, hide, remain silent, calm down, please, etc. to avoid it.
Or choose confrontation, revenge, etc. to fight.
**We think that only when we become better can we be valuable to others, but we ignore that you are valuable just as you are now. **
### Three solution steps
1. **Talk about it. **It doesn’t necessarily mean talking to others, but taking a moment to observe what happened before without judgment.
2. **No big deal. **Now that something has happened, just try to do it well next time, and don’t judge yourself again.
3. **Accept yourself**. Let go of yourself who did that thing in the first place, and let go of yourself who regrets that thing in the past.
> When you say "I should XXX" in your heart, it is very likely that it is not something that comes from your heart.
