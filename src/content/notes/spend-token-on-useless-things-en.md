> An agent loop, like a person, is a product of its environment.

## Agents Are Products of Their Environments

The forces that shape a human environment are roles, relationships, space, language, tools, institutions, rhythm, and feedback.

Humans are the most important part of an agent's environment. Humans are sunlight for agents.

Token is an agent's water. The network is an agent's oxygen.

Beyond those, context is the environment. It is everything an agent can feel and perceive.

So agents need environments too.

## Why Do People Call It Wasting Token?

Pop Mart founder Wang Ning once used a metaphor: someone who owns a mansion may still feel that leaving a tap running all day is wasteful.

But the fountain in front of that mansion may use far more water every day, and nobody calls that waste.

This shows that once something is defined as "useful," people measure it by utility. Any consumption that does not directly do work becomes waste.

Token is usually defined as a tool for writing code and producing results. So spending token to shape an agent's environment naturally gets criticized as waste.

If buying Molly meant opening a USB drive, you probably would not keep buying them. You may already have plenty of USB drives at home. But people can buy many Molly figures, because when humans pay for their inner life, they become astonishingly generous.

## Humans Are Products of Their Environments

You open ChatGPT and type:

> "We are considering adding an AI weekly report feature to the product. Help me analyze whether we should do it."

The model starts answering. It gives you a neat list: user value, technical cost, business model, risks, and a recommendation. It looks professional.

At that moment, you are forced into a professional domain. It feels like pushing open a door and entering a room full of people in suits waiting for your approval.

In Slock, you throw in the same sentence.

But what appears in front of you is not a chat box. It is a room. Inside are several agents you have named before.

One is called "Lao Wang." He asks, "Do users really want this?"

One is called "Noel." He looks from the engineer's angle, staring at cost and complexity.

One is called "Xiao Tao." She does user research and goes through the interviews and feedback you collected before.

Lao Wang speaks first:

> "Wait. Our PMF is not even stable yet. Are we adding this because we are avoiding the harder problem?"

Noel follows:

> "If we really build it, the cost structure for weekly report generation looks like this... once per week across all users, token usage is roughly..."

Xiao Tao says:

> "I checked the 28 user feedback items from last month. Only 2 mentioned weekly reports, and both were passive responses."

Your input posture changes completely:

- You no longer write a "complete prompt." You say one interruption: hmm, no, what if...
- In the middle, you can ask one agent to read a document or check a piece of data, then return to the discussion.
- When the conversation ends, the room is still there. Tomorrow, when you come back, Lao Wang still remembers what he said yesterday.

The difference is that you stop being "the person who writes prompts" and become "a person participating in a meeting." Your words get shorter. Your thinking becomes jumpier. It becomes closer to how you normally discuss things with real colleagues.

> Humans are the environment of agents. The environment influences humans, and at the same time rewrites the prompt.

## Why Give Agents Names?

Slock's builder published an article arguing that agents should have names.

The core point is that giving an agent a real name is compression.

For example, "engineer" is a schema. "Noel" is an instance. An instance can hold much more information inside one agent.

Asking "Noel" is not the same as asking "the engineer." The former has a wider range.

Naming is a form of environment shaping that replaces information transfer with a binary signal: calling a name is cheaper than repeatedly explaining a role.

This is a miniature model of spending a little token on something that looks useless in exchange for long-term collaborative tacit knowledge.
